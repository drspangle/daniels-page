I am a 4th year software engineering bachelors student at the University of Ontario Institute of Technology (UOIT), in Oshawa, Ontario, Canada. sneakyFS File System Design and Implementation

Winter 2012

SneakyFS is a UNIX file system which operates on a simulated hard disk. It uses a hybrid indexed and linked allocation method to efficiently use disk space, support large disks, lengthy directory trees, and large file sizes. It incorporates transparent encryption, journalling, and universal unique identifiers. It incorporates techniques which extend the functionality of the C language beyond its original scope of usage, including object oriented design principles by re-purposing C data structures and pointers. The project won distinction as "Best Project" in the UOIT ENGR 3950 (Operating Systems) course.

Supervisor: Dr. Kamran Sartipi, Associate Professor, UOIT

Work In Progress


IRIS - Incident Recognition and Intelligence System

2013 Undergraduate Capstone Project

The Ministry of Transportation Ontario COMPASS project uses 105 video cameras and inductive coil sensors along major Ontario highways, including the busiest highway in the world (401). Video is analysed by humans, their attention divided between monitoring and administrative tasks. When incidents are detected, they are logged on paper. Logs are used to manually update highway signage, estimate traffic conditions, and direct emergency response. Autonomously recognizing road conditions and hazards, IRIS will improve safety and make surveillance scalable. It will augment human monitoring and automate manual processes. IRIS will monitor incidents as they occur, when a human might miss them. Algorithms will produce better traffic flow statistics through direct observation. Emergency response will improve by computer direction. In the future, driverless vehicles will benefit from traffic updates to enhance route guidance. Drivers will avoid contributing to traffic jams. Logging, analysis, and categorization of incidents will contribute to automated accident reconstruction, improving highway infrastructure planning.

Research and development funding in partnership with The University of Toronto Intelligent Transportation Systems Group, and the Ministry of Transportation Ontario through the One-ITS initiative.

Enhancing the Performance of Genetic Algorithms in Combinatorial Optimization of Large and Difficult Problems

Daniel Smullen, Jonathan Gillett, Joseph Heron

Using variable adaptive mutation rates controlled by 'inbreeding', we show experimental evidence that our new algorithm performs better than conventional genetic algorithm based approaches to combinatorial optimization. In this instance, we use the N-Queens Problem to demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. Collecting datasets from exhaustive experimental runs using SHARCNET, our preliminary findings show an over ten-fold increase in performance with large scale unsolved problems, such as the 32-Queens problem.

How much does metadata reveal about us? An assessment of online privacy.

Daniel Smullen, Jonathan Gillett, Joseph Heron

Metadata has garnered a lot of media attention in recent times due to revelations about international government involvement in mass information collection. Many people perceive that the government and private interests have more information than they should about individuals, but are unclear about the nature of the information, its meaning, and exactly what can be traced back to them. This personally identifiable information is pervasive in routine Internet traffic as people go about their ordinary daily browsing activities, without realizing the extent and impact of third party data collection, which may record all or part of the information users reveal online. Our study seeks to identify Internet users' perceptions about what they are revealing about themselves through their browsing, and compare this to what is truly being revealed - and potentially collected/recorded. A proxy server which collects request information will be used to gather this information from consenting test subjects who connect to it. Collected data will be anonymized, aggregated, and analyzed. Our expectation is that there will be a wide gap in perceptions between the information that users believe they are revealing, and what is actually being collected by third parties.
